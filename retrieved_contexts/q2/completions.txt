When we look at the ApoE alleles, E3 is the most common, with a vast majority of people carrying it. E2 is on the rarer side, which means fewer people have it. E4 is somewhere in the middle. To give you a clearer picture, about 20% of the general population has one E4 allele. Now, when we talk about having two E4 alleles, like you do, only about 2-4% of the population falls into that category.

It's important to note that these percentages just give an overview of the genetic landscape; they don't predict outcomes for individuals. Think of it like this: just because you're part of that 2-4% doesn't mean you're on a set path towards a particular condition. Genes, including E4, are merely one of many factors influencing our health. The presence of E4 does indicate a higher risk for certain conditions, specifically mild cognitive impairment or Alzheimer's-related dementia. But it's essential to remember that "risk" doesn't mean "certainty." It's similar to how some people might have a family history of a condition but never develop it themselves.
